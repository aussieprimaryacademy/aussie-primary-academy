How to Improve Your Child’s Reading Skills at Home Simple reading strategies for Australian parents to build reading confidence, vocabulary and comprehension from Foundation to Year 6. Reading at home does not need to feel like extra schoolwork. A few minutes of relaxed, regular reading can help children build confidence, learn new words and understand stories and information more deeply. The most important thing is to make reading a positive part of everyday life. Whether your child is just beginning to read in Foundation or developing more advanced comprehension skills in Year 6, small daily habits can make a meaningful difference. 1. Make Reading Part of the Everyday Routine Children benefit from regular reading practice. Try creating a simple reading routine that fits naturally into your day. This might be: - reading a story before bed - reading together after school - looking at a book during quiet time - reading a recipe while cooking - reading signs, menus or instructions together The goal is not always to read for a long time. A short, positive reading session is often more effective than a long session that feels tiring or stressful. For younger children, even 10 minutes of reading each day can help build confidence and enjoyment. 2. Let Your Child Choose Some Books Children are more likely to enjoy reading when they have some choice. Encourage your child to choose books, magazines, comics, information books or other reading materials that interest them. A child who loves animals might enjoy: - animal fact books - wildlife stories - books about pets - non-fiction texts about Australian animals A child who enjoys space might prefer: - space books - planet information - science magazines - stories about astronauts Reading interest can be a powerful motivator. The most important question is often not “Is this book difficult enough?” but “Does my child want to read it?” 3. Read Together Reading together gives children an opportunity to hear fluent reading and talk about what they are reading. You can take turns reading: - one page each - one paragraph each - different characters in a story - a sentence at a time For younger readers, you might read most of the story while your child joins in with familiar words. For older children, shared reading can lead to conversations about characters, ideas, information and the author's message. Reading together also shows children that reading is something people do for enjoyment and learning. 4. Talk About the Story Reading is about more than saying the words on the page. Talking about a text helps children develop comprehension and deeper understanding. Before reading, ask: - What do you think this book might be about? - What do you notice on the cover? - What do you already know about this topic? While reading, ask: - What do you think will happen next? - Why do you think the character did that? - What does this word mean? - What information have we learned? After reading, ask: - What was the main idea? - What happened first, next and last? - Which part did you enjoy most? - What was the most interesting thing you learned? These conversations can be simple and natural. They do not need to feel like a test. 5. Build Vocabulary Through Everyday Conversations Children learn new vocabulary through reading, listening and conversation. When your child comes across an unfamiliar word, pause and explore it together. For example: «“The dog was exhausted.”» You might explain: «“Exhausted means very tired.”» Then connect the word to everyday life: «“You might feel exhausted after a very busy day.”» This helps children understand not only the meaning of a word but also how it can be used. You can also encourage your child to: - explain new words in their own words - think of a similar word - think of an opposite word - use the word in a new sentence A strong vocabulary supports both reading comprehension and writing. 6. Help Younger Readers With Phonics Beginning readers often need support connecting letters and sounds. When your child comes across an unfamiliar word, encourage them to: 1. Look at the letters. 2. Say the sounds they know. 3. Blend the sounds together. 4. Look at the sentence for clues. 5. Try the word again. Try to avoid immediately saying every difficult word for your child. Instead, give them time to work it out. Decodable texts and phonics-based reading activities can also provide valuable practice with sound-letter relationships and blending. 7. Encourage Children to Reread Favourite Books Rereading is useful practice. When children read a familiar book again, they can focus on: - reading more smoothly - recognising familiar words - improving expression - understanding the story more deeply - building confidence A book does not become less valuable just because your child has already read it. Favourite books often help children develop fluency because the story and vocabulary become familiar. 8. Read Different Types of Texts Children need experience reading different kinds of texts. Alongside stories, encourage your child to read: - information books - instructions - recipes - signs - maps - labels - menus - comics - magazines - websites designed for children Different texts require different reading skills. For example, a recipe requires children to follow steps in order, while an information text may require them to locate key facts. Reading widely helps children become flexible and confident readers. 9. Use Questions That Encourage Thinking Some questions have a simple answer directly in the text. Other questions encourage children to think more deeply. Try questions such as: Finding information - Who was in the story? - Where did the story happen? - What happened first? Thinking and predicting - What do you think might happen next? - Why do you think the character felt that way? - What clues helped you make that prediction? Making connections - Does this remind you of anything? - Have you ever experienced something similar? - What does this information make you wonder about? The aim is to help children explain their thinking, not simply find the “correct” answer. 10. Help Your Child Build Reading Fluency Reading fluency means reading with increasing accuracy, smoothness and expression. You can support fluency by: - reading aloud to your child - taking turns reading - rereading familiar texts - practising short passages - encouraging natural expression For younger children, repeated reading of a short passage can help build confidence. For older children, reading aloud can help develop expression and understanding of more complex texts. 11. Make Reading a Positive Experience Children can become discouraged if reading always feels like a test. Try to keep reading time relaxed. Praise effort and progress with comments such as: - “You worked hard to figure out that word.” - “I noticed you went back and checked the sentence.” - “Your reading sounded smoother today.” - “That was a thoughtful answer.” - “You made a good connection to the story.” Specific encouragement helps children understand what they are doing well. 12. Practise Little and Often Reading progress is built through regular practice. You do not need to complete a long worksheet or read for an hour every day. A simple routine might include: - Foundation–Year 1: shared reading, phonics and short decodable texts - Years 2–3: reading fluency, vocabulary and simple comprehension - Years 4–6: longer texts, deeper comprehension and critical thinking The best routine is one that your family can maintain consistently. How Worksheets Can Support Reading Practice Reading worksheets can provide structured practice alongside books and everyday reading. A well-designed worksheet might help children practise: - reading comprehension - sequencing events - finding information - vocabulary - phonics - decoding - identifying the main idea - answering questions using evidence from a text Worksheets should support reading — not replace real books and meaningful reading experiences. A Simple Reading Routine for Families Here is an easy routine you can try: Before reading Look at the title and pictures. Ask: “What do you think this might be about?” During reading Pause occasionally to discuss unfamiliar words and important ideas. Ask: “What do you think will happen next?” After reading Talk about the story or information. Ask: “What was the most important thing you learned?” This simple routine helps children practise predicting, understanding vocabulary and thinking about what they have read. Final Thoughts Helping your child become a confident reader does not require complicated activities. Regular reading, relaxed conversations and encouragement can make a powerful difference over time. Read together. Talk about books. Explore new words. Encourage questions. Let children discover texts that interest them. Most importantly, help your child see reading as something they can enjoy, explore and use to understand the world around them. Small reading habits today can help build confident readers for the future.
