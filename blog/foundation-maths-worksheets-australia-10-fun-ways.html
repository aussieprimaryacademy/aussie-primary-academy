Why Early Maths Matters

The first years of school build the foundation for everything that comes later. Children who develop confidence with numbers early often find addition, subtraction, multiplication and problem solving much easier in later primary school.

Early maths isn't about memorising difficult sums. It's about learning to:

  • recognise numbers
  • count objects accurately
  • compare quantities
  • identify patterns
  • understand shapes
  • solve simple everyday problems

When children enjoy maths, they're more willing to keep trying—even when something feels challenging.

10 Simple Maths Activities You Can Do at Home

These activities use ordinary household objects and everyday situations. The goal is not to create another formal lesson. Instead, look for small opportunities to notice, discuss and explore maths together.

01

Count Everything

Count toys, apples, socks, books or stairs. The more children count real objects, the stronger their number sense becomes.

Try this: Ask your child to count objects in a group and check whether the final number tells them how many objects there are altogether.

02

Play Number Hunts

Choose a number and ask your child to find it around the house. Examples include house numbers, clocks, calendars, food packets and books.

Try this: Choose one number and see how many different places your child can find it.

03

Sort Household Objects

Sort buttons, blocks or toys by colour, size, shape or type. Sorting develops early mathematical thinking.

Try this: Ask your child whether the same objects could be sorted in a different way.

04

Build Patterns

Create simple patterns using shapes, objects or colours. For example: 🔵🔴🔵🔴.

Try this: Stop partway through the pattern and ask your child what should come next.

05

Cook Together

Cooking naturally teaches counting, measuring, comparing and sequencing. Children can practise maths while helping with everyday tasks.

Try this: Count ingredients, compare amounts or talk about what needs to happen first, next and last.

06

Play Dice Games

Dice games can help children recognise numbers, count on and compare quantities.

Try this: Roll two dice and ask which number is greater, or count the total number of dots.

07

Use Printable Worksheets

Short worksheet sessions help children practise skills they've already explored through play.

Try this: Choose one short worksheet that matches something your child has already been practising.

Explore free Foundation Maths worksheets for extra practice.

08

Read Number Books

Many picture books introduce counting and early maths through stories that children enjoy.

Try this: Pause during a story and count objects, compare groups or notice numbers in the illustrations.

09

Ask Everyday Maths Questions

Ask questions like "Which pile has more?", "How many plates do we need?" or "What shape is this sign?".

Try this: Give your child time to explain how they know their answer.

10

Celebrate Effort

Praise persistence rather than perfect answers. Children develop confidence when they know mistakes are part of learning.

Try this: Praise the strategy, effort or explanation your child used—not only the final answer.

Simple Tips for Parents

10–15 Minutes Can Be Enough

Young children usually benefit more from short, regular practice than long study sessions.

Play First

Real objects, games and conversations can help children understand a concept before they practise it on paper.

Praise Effort

Confidence grows when children understand that mistakes and persistence are part of learning.

One Activity at a Time

You do not need to complete every activity in one day. Choose one small opportunity to explore maths together.
