Why Early Maths Matters

The first years of school build the foundation for everything that comes later. Children who develop confidence with numbers early often find addition, subtraction, multiplication and problem solving much easier in later primary school.

Early maths isn't about memorising difficult sums. It's about learning to:

  • Recognise numbers
  • Count objects accurately
  • Compare quantities
  • Identify patterns
  • Understand shapes
  • Solve simple everyday problems

When children enjoy maths, they're more willing to keep trying—even when something feels challenging.

10 Simple Maths Activities You Can Do at Home

These simple activities help children practise early mathematical thinking through everyday play, conversation and practical experiences.

1. Count Everything

Count toys, apples, socks, books or stairs. The more children count real objects, the stronger their number sense becomes.

Try this: Ask your child to count a group of objects, then check the answer by touching each object once.

2. Play Number Hunts

Choose a number and ask your child to find it around the house. Examples include house numbers, clocks, calendars, food packets and books.

Try this: Choose one number and see how many times your child can find it during the day.

3. Sort Household Objects

Sort buttons, blocks or toys by colour, size, shape or type. Sorting develops early mathematical thinking.

Ask: How could we sort these objects in a different way?

4. Build Patterns

Create simple patterns using shapes, objects or colours. Ask your child what comes next and explain how they know.

Try this: Make a repeating pattern with two objects, then invite your child to continue it.

5. Cook Together

Cooking naturally teaches counting, measuring, comparing and sequencing. Children love helping in the kitchen while practising maths without even realising it.

Ask: What do we need first? Which container holds more? How many pieces do we have?

6. Play Dice Games

Dice games improve number recognition, counting on and comparing quantities.

Try this: Roll two dice and ask which number is greater. Older Foundation learners can count the dots on both dice.

7. Use Printable Worksheets

Short worksheet sessions help children practise skills they've already explored through play.

You can explore our free Foundation Maths worksheets and learning resources for extra practice.

8. Read Number Books

Many picture books introduce counting and early maths through stories that children enjoy.

Try this: Pause during the story and ask your child to count objects, notice numbers or predict what might happen next.

9. Ask Everyday Maths Questions

Ask questions like "Which pile has more?", "How many plates do we need?" or "What shape is this sign?".

Small conversations build mathematical thinking every day.

10. Celebrate Effort

Praise persistence rather than perfect answers. Children develop confidence when they know mistakes are part of learning.

Remember: The goal is to build confidence and curiosity, not to get every answer right immediately.

Tips for Parents

Children don't need hours of maths practice. Instead, try:

  • 10–15 minute sessions
  • Regular praise
  • Fun games
  • Real-life maths activities
  • One worksheet at a time

Consistency is much more important than long study sessions.
